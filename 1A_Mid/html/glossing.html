Terms and Their Historical Definitions

Reign

The period during which a king or queen rules a kingdom. In the case of the Lighthouse of Alexandria, it was built during the Ptolemaic Kingdom, a Hellenistic dynasty that ruled Egypt after the death of Alexander the Great.

Lighthouse

A tall tower with a light source at the top, used to guide ships. The Lighthouse of Alexandria was one of the most famous and advanced of its time.

Wonders

Extraordinary and grand works, considered the greatest human achievements of a certain era. The Lighthouse of Alexandria was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.

Ancient World

A period in history that extends from Classical Antiquity to the fall of the Western Roman Empire. The Lighthouse of Alexandria is one of the most important monuments from this period.

Navigators

People who travel the seas, guiding ships. The Lighthouse of Alexandria was essential for guiding navigators arriving at the port of Alexandria.

Island of Pharos

A small island located off the Egyptian coast, where the Lighthouse of Alexandria was built. The island was connected to the mainland by a long causeway.

Ptolemy

A Greek dynasty that ruled Egypt after the death of Alexander the Great. The Ptolemies were responsible for the construction of the Lighthouse of Alexandria.

Architecture

The art and technique of designing and constructing buildings. The architecture of the Lighthouse of Alexandria was innovative for its time, utilizing advanced materials and construction techniques.

Sostratus of Cnidus

A Greek architect responsible for the design and construction of the Lighthouse of Alexandria.

Height

The vertical distance between the base and the highest point of an object. The Lighthouse of Alexandria was one of the tallest structures in the ancient world, standing between 120 and 137 meters tall.

Square Base

The lower part of the lighthouse, with a square shape.

Octagonal Tower

The central part of the lighthouse, with an octagonal shape (eight sides).

Marble

A metamorphic rock, often used in construction for its beauty and durability. Although it's not confirmed that the Lighthouse of Alexandria was entirely built with marble, this stone may have been used in some parts of the structure.

Flame

A source of intense light, usually produced by combustion. At the top of the Lighthouse of Alexandria, a large flame was kept burning to guide ships.

Poseidon

The Greek god of the seas. In Greek mythology, Poseidon was often associated with lighthouses and other coastal structures.

Greek Mythology

A collection of myths and legends from ancient Greece. Greek mythology played an important role in people's lives at the time and influenced the construction of monuments like the Lighthouse of Alexandria.

Granite

An igneous rock, very resistant and hard. Granite may have been used in the construction of the Lighthouse of Alexandria, along with other stones such as limestone.

Limestone

A sedimentary rock, formed from the remains of shells and other marine organisms. Limestone was one of the main materials used in the construction of the Lighthouse of Alexandria.

Mirrors

Polished surfaces that reflect light. It is believed that mirrors were used in the Lighthouse of Alexandria to intensify the light from the flame and increase its visibility.

Earthquake

A violent shaking of the Earth's surface, caused by movements of tectonic plates. Several theories suggest that earthquakes were responsible for the destruction of the Lighthouse of Alexandria over the centuries.
